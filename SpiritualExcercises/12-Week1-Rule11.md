### Riviera United Methodist Church
**Thursday Night Small Group**
**The Spiritual Exercises of St. Ignatius of Loyola: Week 1, Rule 11**
- Remember:
  - Desolation is **not** spiritual *losing*.
  - Consolation is **not** spiritual *winning*.
- We need to remain detached from emotional scorekeeping.
- The symmetry of the rules:
  - In Consolation -> humility
  - In Desolation -> confidence in God's grace.
- Always
  - God's grace and mercy are primary
  - We are **always dependent** on God.
  - We are **never abandoned** by God, no matter what we 'feel.'

The text of Rule 11:
> Eleventh Rule (Spiritual Consolation and Spiritual Desolation: Finding Our Balance). The eleventh: let one who is consoled seek to humble himself and lower himself as much as he can, thinking of how little he is capable in the time of desolation without such grace or consolation. On the contrary, let one who is in desolation think that he can do much with God’s sufficient grace to resist all his enemies, taking strength in his Creator and Lord. (SE 324) 

**The Eleventh rule reminds us to remain humble,**
- because consolation is a gift from God, not based on our merits.

**The Eleventh rule is about striking a balance between consolation and desolation.**
- Spiritual memory is very important; not emotionalism.
  - Always interpret you current spiritual state through the lens of God's grace, not your emotions.
  - We want to prevent spiritual amnesia
  - in consolation: remember how you felt in desolation.
    - name the feelings; don't judge yourself for having feelings.  Learn from them.
    - don't fall into scrupulosity.
  - in desolation: remember the confidence you had in God's grace and mercy.
    - avoid moralism or triumphalism.

**The Eleventh rule is a time to recognize the grace God gives us in desolation to resist all activity of the enemy.**
- In the previous time of desolation, even if you felt abandoned, sufficient grace was present - though hidden - enabling you to remain faithful.
  - We may, or may not have faltered.  That is not the point.
  - We should learn from that time: good or bad.
  - Where, and how did we run to God to draw on His grace and strength?
  - Where, and how did we **NOT** run to God to draw on His grace and strength?
  - Reflect on how difficult you consider the last desolation.
    - Reflect on the fact that God made enough grace available to you to see you through it.
- A season of consolation is a time to renew our fidelity to God.
  - It is a time to strengthen our connection to the branch: the source, God, who sustains us.
  - It is a time to recognize our reliance on God's grace at all times.
    - We also need grace in time of consolation to see clearly our relationship with God.
- The enemy can tempt us in a time of consolation as well
  - This connects with the week 2 rules.
  - by putting the thought in our mind that we can relax a little.
    - False security.
    - subtle spiritual pride.
  - That would be akin to allowing a little separation from the branch that sustains us.
  - Or we could begin to think that *our* efforts were sufficient to see us through the last desolation,
    - and that we now have our reward.
- Holy Boldness! especially in desolation.
  - "...let one who is in desolation think that he can do much with God’s sufficient grace to resist all..."
  - It is militant, not passive.
    - desolation does not call for passive survival.
  - Active resistance, relying on, leaning on, God's grace.
    - Stand firm
    - Resist/fight actively
    - Act
    - Trust
- Humility before God is necessary in both consolation and desolation.

**Scripture Annotations for Rule 11:** deeply relevant passages:
1. 2 Corinthians 12:9–10 - “My grace is sufficient for you… for when I am weak, then I am strong.” This is practically Rule 11 summarized.
2. 1 Corinthians 10:12 - “Let anyone who thinks that he stands take heed lest he fall.” Consolation → humility.
3. Philippians 4:13 - “I can do all things through Him who strengthens me.” Desolation → confidence in God’s strengthening grace.
4. John 15:5 - “Apart from me you can do nothing.” Consolation → humility and dependence. The “branch” imagery — this is the perfect anchor text.
5. James 4:6 - “God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.” Consolation without humility becomes dangerous.

**Jesuit Insights**:
- St. Ignatius of Loyola - From a letter: “Act as if everything depended on you; trust as if everything depended on God.” That is Rule 11 in both halves.
- Pedro Arrupe - “We must not seek consolation, but the God of consolation.” This guards against spiritual attachment.
- Hans Urs von Balthasar - Though not a Jesuit, he writes: “Grace does not abolish weakness; it makes it luminous.” That is Rule 11’s theology.

**Desert Fathers & Mothers** The desert tradition resonates deeply with Rule 11.
- Abba Anthony - “Expect temptation to your last breath.” Desolation is normal — not failure.
- Evagrius Ponticus - “If you are a theologian, you truly pray. If you truly pray, you are a theologian.” Consolation must remain prayerful dependence — not self-congratulation.
- Abba Moses the Black - When asked about progress: “Go, sit in your cell, and your cell will teach you everything.” Stay. Remain. Resist. Trust grace.
