**The Spiritual Exercises of St. Ignatius of Loyola: Week 1, Rule 12**
- Remember:
  - Desolation is **not** spiritual *losing*.
  - Consolation is **not** spiritual *winning*.
- We need to remain detached from emotional scorekeeping.
- The symmetry of the rules:
  - In Consolation -> humility
  - In Desolation -> confidence in God's grace.
- Always
  - God's grace and mercy are primary
  - We are **always dependent** on God.
  - We are **never abandoned** by God, no matter what we 'feel.'

The text of Rule 12:
> Twelfth Rule (Standing Firm in the Beginnings). …it is proper to the enemy to weaken and loose heart, fleeing and ceasing his temptations when the person who is exercising himself in spiritual things confronts the temptations of the enemy firmly, doing what is diametrically opposed to them; and, on the contrary, if the person who is exercising himself begins to be afraid and lose heart in suffering the temptations, there is no beast so fierce on the face of the earth as the enemy of human nature in following out his damnable intention with such growing malice. (SE 325) 

Rewritten:
> The enemy of our souls is bold only when we are hesitant. When confronted with courage and firm resolve, he retreats. But when he finds fear or discouragement, he becomes aggressive and relentless. Therefore, when temptation first arises, oppose it decisively and with confidence in God’s grace.

**The Twelfth rule is very straight forward, and reminds us to not be passive when temptation strikes.**
- This rule says that much is dependent on our attitude to consolation and desolation.
- If we act passive and/or fearful the enemy of our soul will attack with even more vehemence.
  - We must remember God's grace.
  - We must remember God is still near.
- If we are brave and bold in the face of desolation, temptation, and adversity,
  - the enemy cannot help but back away.
  - **HOWEVER**, we must remember it is only God's grace and mercy that allows us to be bold and brave.
    - it is not our power and might the enemy is afraid of.
  - We have to maintain our humility.
- For me, the mental of image of child standing behind their father when faced with something frightening is a help to remember that it is God who defends us.
- Another image is mentally imagining the crucifixion and the resurrection.
  - Jesus' victory breaks our chains to sin and death.
  - By and through that victory we can stand firm in desolation or when tempted in consolation.

**Scripture Annotations for Rule 12:**
1. James 4:7 “Submit yourselves therefore to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you.”
   - This is almost a direct summary of Ignatius’ rule. The order matters:
     - First: submit to God.
     - Then: resist.
     - Result: the enemy flees.
   - Ignatius assumes the same dynamic — resistance rooted in dependence on God.
2. 1 Peter 5:8–9 “Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion… Resist him, firm in your faith.”
   - Peter uses predator imagery similar to what we might use in a modern rewrite. The emphasis is not panic, but firmness in faith. The lion retreats when it meets resistance.
3. Ephesians 6:10–13 “Put on the whole armor of God… that you may be able to stand.”
   - Notice the repeated word: stand.
     - Not chase. Not panic. Not collapse.
     - **Stand**.
   - Ignatius’ rule is essentially tactical spiritual warfare drawn straight from Paul’s imagery.
4. 2 Timothy 2:22 “Flee youthful passions and pursue righteousness…”
   - Ignatius says to act diametrically opposed to temptation. Paul says the same:
     - Don’t negotiate.
     - Don’t analyze endlessly.
     - Flee and pursue the opposite good.
5. Genesis 4:7 “Sin is crouching at the door… but you must rule over it.”
   - This is perhaps the earliest articulation of Rule 12 in Scripture.
     - Sin crouches (passive but ready). If you do not master it immediately, it masters you.
6. Hebrews 3:13 “Exhort one another every day… that none of you may be hardened by the deceitfulness of sin.”
   - Temptation hardens progressively. The longer it is entertained, the stronger it becomes.

**Jesuit Sources Relevant to Rule 12**
1. Peter Faber: Work: Memoriale (Spiritual Diary)
   - See entries from 1542–1546 (various reflections on discernment of spirits).
     - Particularly entries discussing “first movements” of spirits and vigilance over thoughts.
   - Faber repeatedly emphasizes attentiveness to the beginnings of spiritual movements. For example, in reflections from 1542 (spring entries), he notes the importance of observing interior changes at their inception before they gather force.
   - While not a single quotable aphorism, the theme appears throughout the Memoriale:
     - Spiritual discernment must begin with noticing the first interior stirring.
2. Luis de la Puente: Work: Meditations on the Mysteries of Our Holy Faith
   - Particularly in meditations on temptation and spiritual combat (Vol. 1, early sections).
   - He discusses how temptations must be resisted at their first movement or they increase in strength. The teaching appears in sections treating:
     - The combat with passions
     - Resistance to interior suggestions
3. Jean-Pierre de Caussade: Work: Abandonment to Divine Providence
   - Book I, Chapters 1–3 (on fidelity to the present moment)
     - Later letters addressing discouragement and temptation
   - Caussade emphasizes:
     - Grace is present in the current moment.
     - Fidelity must be immediate, not delayed.

**Desert Fathers & Mothers (Primary Sources)**
1. Anthony the Great: 
   - Work: Life of Antony by Athanasius of Alexandria - Especially chapters 5–13 (Antony’s early temptations)
   - Antony teaches that demons lose strength when confronted with faith and boldness.
     - In Chapter 13, he tells monks not to fear demonic assaults because their power diminishes when mocked or resisted with confidence in Christ.
2. Evagrius Ponticus:
   - Works:
     - Praktikos (especially chapters 6–14)
     - On Thoughts
   - Evagrius explicitly teaches: **Do not converse with tempting thoughts.**
   - In Praktikos (early chapters), he explains that thoughts grow stronger when entertained and must be rejected immediately. This is perhaps the clearest Desert parallel to Ignatius’ Rule 12.
3. John Cassian: 
   - Work: Conferences
     - Conference 5 (on the eight principal vices)
     - Conference 7 (on the spirit of fornication)
   - Also Institutes, Book V
   - Cassian compares temptation to a spark that becomes a blaze if not extinguished early.
   - In Conference 5, he describes how vice begins with a small suggestion that grows through consent.
4. Syncletica of Alexandria: 
   - Source: Sayings of the Desert Fathers (Apophthegmata Patrum)
     - Alphabetical Collection, “Syncletica”
   - She speaks of early struggle leading to later peace and of resisting passions at their outset.
   - Her sayings emphasize endurance in the early fight again matching Ignatius’ insistence on firmness at the beginning.
5. Abba Poemen: 
   - Source: Sayings of the Desert Fathers
     - Alphabetical Collection, “Poemen”
     - Multiple sayings deal with guarding the heart and cutting off thoughts before they mature.
       - While not framed in combat imagery, the principle is clear: **Unattended thoughts grow.**
